# Full PM Interview Curriculum

Based on *Cracking the PM Interview* by McDowell & Bavaro.

---

## Module 1: The PM Role & Mindset
**Source:** Chapters 1–2

### Lessons
1. **What is a PM?** — The "mini-CEO" analogy and why it's incomplete. Leading without authority. Sitting at the intersection of tech, business, and design.
2. **PM Functions** — Research & Planning, Design, Implement & Test, Release. What the PM does at each stage.
3. **Types of Products** — Shipped software vs. online software. Consumer vs. B2B. How each changes the PM's daily work.
4. **PM Myths** — Common misconceptions candidates hold (PMs just manage timelines, PMs are just customer advocates, etc.)

### Key Concepts to Teach
- A PM must *influence without authority* — engineers are building the product, not the PM
- The PM is the advocate for the customer, not just a stakeholder traffic controller
- OKRs as the PM's success-definition tool
- A/B testing, dogfooding, usability studies — how PMs gather evidence
- The PM lifecycle diagram (Research → Design → Implement → Release is cyclical, not linear)

### Teaching Example
Walk through: "Imagine you're PM for a new messaging feature. What does your week look like across the lifecycle phases?"

### Socratic Check
"If an engineer on your team says 'I don't think we should build this feature,' what do you do? You can't fire them."

### Common Mistakes
- Candidates say PMs "approve" or "direct" engineering work — they don't have that authority
- Conflating the PM with the project manager or program manager

### Practice Readiness Signal
The user can explain PM functions across the lifecycle with concrete examples, and articulate how PM varies across product types.

## Module 2: Companies & Interview Styles
**Source:** Chapters 3, 6, 10

### Lessons
1. **How the PM Role Varies** — Between companies and team cultures, between startup and large company, between consumer and enterprise
2. **Company Research Framework** — Product, Strategy, Culture, Role, The Questions (Ch 10)
3. **Your Target Companies** — Build a rubric for the specific companies you're interviewing at

### Company Research Framework (Ch 10)
Before any interview, research:
- **The Product** — features, UX, gaps, recent launches
- **The Strategy** — competitive landscape, revenue model, growth bets
- **The Culture** — values, how decisions get made, what the company cares about
- **The Role** — what the team works on, who you'd work with, what success looks like
- **Key Metrics** — MAU, revenue, growth rate, conversion

Use this framework to build a rubric for every company you're targeting. If you share your target company, your tutor will help you apply it.

### Well-Known Company Profiles (Canonical Examples)
These profiles are drawn from the book and represent how major tech companies were known to interview. Use them as baseline examples and supplement with current research.

**Google:** Analytical, data-driven. PMs are expected to love data and run experiments. Strong engineering culture — PMs must earn engineers' respect. Bottom-up decision-making. Estimation questions are common.

**Microsoft:** Varies heavily by team. Can involve pseudocode. More project-management-oriented on some teams. Product specs matter.

**Meta (Facebook):** Hacker culture. Entrepreneurial drive. Interview "hats": Technical/Logical, Design, Futurist, Guru. Coding often expected.

**Apple:** Culture fit is paramount. Passion for the product above all. Heavy design influence. Expect many interview rounds.

**Amazon:** 14 Leadership Principles drive everything. "Bar raiser" as special interviewer. Deep resume interrogation — every bullet will be challenged. Customer obsession is core. Pricing and metrics are important.

**Twitter/X:** You must deeply "get" the platform. Technical design questions are possible.

**Dropbox:** Technical and founding experience valued. Attention to detail matters.

> **Note:** Company interview styles evolve. Always supplement these profiles with recent candidate reports (e.g., Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, Blind) before your actual interview.

### Your Target Companies
At the start of your sessions, share the companies you're interviewing at. Your tutor will help you build or customize a rubric for each one using the Company Research Framework above.

### Socratic Check
"Pick one company you're targeting. Name 3 things you know about how they interview for PM roles, and what you'd need to research further."

### Common Mistakes
- Treating every company the same — each has a distinct culture and question style
- Going to Amazon without knowing the Leadership Principles cold
- Not researching the specific team or product area you're interviewing for
- Underestimating how much company-specific passion matters at product-centric companies

## Module 3: Define Yourself — The 6 Self-Definition Questions
**Source:** Chapter 11

### Lessons
1. **"Tell Me About Yourself" (The Pitch)** — 2-minute structured story connecting background → accomplishments → why this role
2. **"Why do you want to work here?"** — Show company research, relevant experience, or real passion. Not "great culture."
3. **"Why should we hire you?"** — Structure it: why you're a good PM + why you fit this space + why this company/culture
4. **"Why are you leaving your current job?"** — Keep it positive. Focus on what you're moving toward, not what you're escaping.
5. **"What do you like to do in your spare time?"** — Show initiative, passion, leadership — backed by concrete evidence
6. **"Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"** — Ambition + realism + alignment with this role
7. **"What are your strengths and weaknesses?"** — Genuine strengths backed by evidence. Genuine weaknesses, not disguised positives.

### The Pitch Framework
Structure: Background context → Key accomplishments (connected, with numbers) → Why this role

Rules:
- ~2 minutes (1–2x longer than interviewer's intro)
- Cohesive story, not a resume read-out
- Connect the dots between experiences
- End with *why this specific role*

### Weaknesses Rule
The old "I work too hard" trick is dead. Give a genuine weakness, then show what you've done to compensate. Evidence of self-awareness matters more than the weakness itself.

### Socratic Check
"Draft your 2-minute pitch right now. I'll give feedback on story cohesion, concreteness, and whether you'd sell the interviewer."

### Common Mistakes
- Pitch that's too long or just recites the resume
- "Why do you want to work here?" answered with "great culture / great people" — too generic
- Weaknesses that are transparently disguised strengths

### Practice Readiness Signal
The user has drafted and refined their personal pitch. They have prepared answers for all 6 questions, with concrete evidence in each.

## Module 4: Behavioral Story Questions (STAR Method)
**Source:** Chapter 12

### Lessons
1. **Why behavioral questions are asked** — Verify resume claims. Predict future behavior from past behavior. Test communication skills.
2. **The STAR Framework** — Situation, Task, Action, Result
3. **Types of behavioral questions** — Leadership, conflict, failure, collaboration, prioritization, data-driven decisions, influencing without authority

### STAR in Detail
- **S**ituation: Brief context (company, team, what was happening)
- **T**ask: What you specifically were responsible for
- **A**ction: What *you* did — be specific, use first-person singular, not "we"
- **R**esult: Quantified outcome + what you learned

### Key Behavioral Categories to Prep
1. A time you led without authority
2. A time you made a data-driven decision
3. A time you failed or a product flopped
4. A time you had to prioritize ruthlessly
5. A time you had to push back on stakeholders
6. A time you shipped something successfully despite obstacles

### Common Mistakes
- Stories that say "we did X" instead of "I did X" — interviewers can't assess your contribution
- Results that aren't quantified ("the team was happy" is weak; "retention improved 12%" is strong)
- Stories that ramble — keep STAR tight (2–3 minutes max per story)
- Answering with hypotheticals — "what I would do" instead of "what I did"

### Socratic Check
"Tell me about a time you had to convince an engineer to build something they didn't want to build. Walk me through it in STAR format."

### Practice Readiness Signal
The user has 6 STAR stories prepared — each with specific numbers and outcomes. They can tell any of them in under 3 minutes.

## Module 5: Estimation Questions
**Source:** Chapter 13

### Lessons
1. **Why estimation questions exist** — Testing problem-solving process and quantitative comfort. Not about the "right answer."
2. **The 8-Step Framework**
3. **Math tricks** — Rule of 72, orders of magnitude, rounding strategy
4. **Numbers cheat sheet** — Key facts to memorize
5. **Common estimation question types**

### The 8-Step Framework
1. **Clarify the question** — Repeat it back, ask about ambiguities (profit vs. revenue? US only? which time period?)
2. **Catalog what you know** — What facts do you have? What do you need?
3. **Make an equation** — Write down the formula before plugging in numbers
4. **Think about edge cases** — What sources did you miss? What assumptions are shaky?
5. **Break it down** — Solve each component of the equation separately
6. **Review & state assumptions** — Tell the interviewer every assumption you're making, and why
7. **Do the math** — Round aggressively. Label units.
8. **Sanity check** — Does the answer make intuitive sense?

### Numbers Cheat Sheet
| Fact | Value |
|------|-------|
| US Population | 300 million |
| Average people/household (US) | 3 |
| US Households | 100 million |
| Life expectancy (US) | 80 years |
| World population | 7 billion |
| Hours in a year | ~9,000 |
| Minutes in a year | ~500,000 |

### Math Tricks
- **Rule of 72:** Time to double = 72 ÷ annual growth rate %
- **Orders of magnitude:** digits(a) + digits(b) should equal digits(a×b) ± 1. If not, check your math.
- **Round aggressively:** Use 300M not 314M. Use 10K not 9,843.
- **Label units always:** Don't lose track of what your number represents

### Common Mistakes
- Jumping to a number without writing the equation first
- Not stating assumptions clearly
- Forgetting the sanity check
- Getting intimidated by "I'm bad at math" — confidence matters

### Socratic Check
"How much revenue does Spotify generate from ads per year in the US? Walk me through your equation before doing any math."

### Practice Readiness Signal
The user can reliably lay out an equation before doing any math, state assumptions clearly, and sanity-check results. At least 2 estimation problems worked through together.

## Module 6: Product Design Questions
**Source:** Chapter 14 — Type 1

### Lessons
1. **What the interviewer is really testing** — Can you think like a PM? Do you start with users?
2. **The 7-Step Design Framework**
3. **Worked example: Design an alarm clock for the blind**
4. **Feature prioritization** — How to evaluate and rank features you propose

### The 7-Step Design Framework
1. **Ask clarifying questions** — Understand scope. Who is the user? What context?
2. **Provide a structure** — Signal to the interviewer how you'll approach this
3. **Identify users and customers** — Who uses this? Who pays for it? Multiple users = multiple needs.
4. **What are the use cases and goals?** — What tasks do users need to accomplish? What's their underlying motivation?
5. **How well does the current product serve those use cases?** — What are the biggest gaps?
6. **What features would improve those weak spots?** — Tie features explicitly back to user needs. Don't propose features you personally want.
7. **Wrap up** — Summarize your proposed solution clearly

### Key Principle
The question is never "what product would YOU want?" It's "what product would the USER want?" Starting with features before users is the #1 mistake.

### Common Mistakes
- Jumping to feature ideas without identifying users first
- Proposing features without tying them to specific use cases
- Not using the whiteboard / not being structured
- Forgetting secondary users (e.g., the parent in a kids' product)

### Socratic Check
"Design a better elevator for a hospital. Walk me through steps 1–3 only before we continue."

### Practice Readiness Signal
The user can execute the 7-step framework in order, spontaneously identify multiple user types, and resist the urge to jump to features.

## Module 7: Product Improvement & Favorite Product
**Source:** Chapter 14 — Types 2 & 3

### Lessons
1. **Improving a product** — Same as design, but start from an existing product's weaknesses
2. **Favorite product** — How to structure a product love story into a structured critique
3. **Metrics for improvement** — What metrics signal whether your improvement worked?

### Product Improvement Structure
1. Identify the product's goal and target user
2. Map the key user journeys
3. Assess where the current product fails (what are the biggest pain points?)
4. Propose improvements that directly address those pain points
5. Discuss metrics you'd track to validate success

### Favorite Product Structure
1. What is the product's goal?
2. Who are the target users?
3. What metrics does this product excel at? Where does it struggle?
4. What you love about it — tied to specific UX decisions
5. What you'd change — backed by user pain points and metrics

### Preparation Tip
Have 2–3 "favorite products" you know deeply: metrics, UX strengths, gaps, and concrete things you'd change. These become go-to examples across many question types.

### Socratic Check
"Pick any app you use every day. Walk me through its key metrics, what it does well, and one thing you'd change — in that order."

## Module 8: Case Questions & Business Frameworks
**Source:** Chapter 15

### Lessons
1. **PM case vs. consulting case** — PMs rely on instinct + structure, not data requests. Drive the interview forward.
2. **AIDA / REAN** — Customer purchase decision frameworks
3. **Marketing Mix (4 Ps)** — Product, Price, Promotion, Place
4. **SWOT Analysis** — Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats
5. **Five Cs** — Company, Competitors, Customers, Collaborators, Climate
6. **Porter's 5 Forces** — Industry analysis framework
7. **Make your own framework** — When to blend or build from scratch

### Framework Quick Reference → see `references/frameworks.md`

### Key Teaching Point
Frameworks are structure tools, not formulas to recite. An interviewer doesn't care if you named the framework. They care that your answer is *structured, thorough, and reasoned*. When in doubt, build your own structure and explain why you organized it that way.

### PM vs. Consultant Case
- Consultants ask for data; PMs use instinct
- PMs drive the interview, don't ride it
- PMs think about execution and implementation, not just analysis
- "I'd need more data to answer that" is often the wrong move for a PM candidate

### Socratic Check
"Should Spotify launch a social network feature? Walk me through how you'd structure this decision."

### Practice Readiness Signal
The user can walk through a strategic decision with a structured approach, blending relevant frameworks naturally without reciting them.

## Module 9: Metrics & Analytics
**Source:** Chapter 15 — Product Metrics section

### Lessons
1. **Types of metrics** — User acquisition, activity, conversion & retention, revenue
2. **AARRR (Pirate Metrics)** — Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Referral, Revenue
3. **Diagnosing a metric drop** — How to think through a sudden change in a KPI
4. **A/B testing basics** — Experiment vs. control, measuring success metrics, rollout
5. **Cohort analysis** — Segmenting by time, geography, behavior

### Key Metrics Categories
**User Acquisition:** Total users, active users, where users come from, referral rate, channel effectiveness

**Activity:** Feature adoption, workflow completion rates, engagement metrics (DAU/MAU)

**Conversion & Retention:** Free-to-paid conversion rate, churn rate, retention curves, NPS

**Revenue:** ARPU (average revenue per user), LTV (lifetime value), CAC (customer acquisition cost), MRR

### Diagnosing a Metric Drop Framework
When a metric drops, work through:
1. Is the data correct? (sampling error, tracking bug?)
2. Did something change in the product? (new feature, A/B test?)
3. Is it external? (competitor launch, seasonality, news event?)
4. Is it a specific segment? (new users vs. old, mobile vs. desktop?)
5. What's the root cause? (propose hypotheses, ranked by likelihood)

### Common Mistakes
- Measuring total volume instead of rate of change
- Not breaking metrics down by cohorts
- Confusing correlation with causation in A/B results

### Socratic Check
"Your DAU dropped 15% last week. Walk me through how you'd diagnose the cause."

## Module 10: Coding Questions for PMs
**Source:** Chapter 16

### Lessons
1. **What's actually expected** — Not leetcode. Algorithm thinking, pseudocode comfort, basic data structures
2. **Key data structures to know conceptually** — Arrays, hash tables, trees, queues/stacks
3. **Algorithm thinking** — Breaking problems into steps, recognizing patterns
4. **When coding is tested** — Google, Meta (sometimes), Microsoft (some teams)

### What to Know Cold
- **Arrays** — when and why to use them
- **Hash tables** — O(1) lookup, use cases (frequency counting, caching)
- **Binary trees** — hierarchy, search, traversal
- **Queues/Stacks** — FIFO vs. LIFO, use cases

### Teaching Approach
Focus on *why* each structure exists and *what problem it solves*, not on memorizing syntax. PMs demonstrate they can think algorithmically and communicate technical decisions, not that they can code production software.

### Common Mistakes
- Panicking and saying "I can't code" — confidence and process matter more than syntax
- Not breaking the problem into steps before writing anything
- Forgetting to mention time/space complexity tradeoffs even at a high level

## Module 11: Resume & Cover Letter
**Source:** Chapters 7–9

### Lessons
1. **The 15-Second Rule** — A resume is skimmed, not read. Optimize for the skim.
2. **The 5 Resume Rules** — Shorter, bullets not blobs, accomplishments not responsibilities, good template, don't skip the best stuff
3. **PM-Specific Attributes** — Passion for technology, initiative, leadership, impact, technical skills, attention to detail
4. **Cover Letter Structure** — The template from Ch 9
5. **What to Include and Exclude** — GPA rules, social media, activities, projects, awards

### The 5 Resume Rules
1. **Shorter is better** — 1 page for <10 years experience
2. **Bullets not blobs** — No bullet longer than 2 lines; 50%+ should be 1 line
3. **Accomplishments not responsibilities** — "Led X, which resulted in Y% improvement" beats "Responsible for X"
4. **Good template** — No gimmicks. Two-column. Easy to skim.
5. **Don't skip the best stuff** — Unfinished projects, side work, impressive hobbies all count if they're relevant

### PM Resume Test
For each bullet, ask: "What would my team say are the 5 most impactful things I did?" If a bullet doesn't answer that, reconsider it.

### Socratic Check
"Give me your 3 most recent resume bullets. I'll help you rewrite them from responsibilities to accomplishments."
